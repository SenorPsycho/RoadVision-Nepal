# Failure Analysis of Classical Lane Detection on Kathmandu Roads

## Overview
This document is the failure analysis of classical lane detection pipeline on unstructured, crowded and non-marked urban roads of Kathmandu, Nepal. Using a US-based scenario for pipeline benchmark, this examines the pipeline's performance on urban roads, reinforcing the idea that the failure is fundamental, not a tuning problem where standard environmental assumptions do not hold.

This document also explores potential adaptations that could improve pipeline robustness in unstructured road environments.

## Pipeline Assumptions vs Environment Reality

| Assumption | Highway Reality | Kathmandu Reality |
|------------|------------------|---------------------|
| Painted lane markings provide high-contrast edges for detection | Clean yellow marking on either side of the road and dashed white lines in the middle | No real detectable edges on the road surface |
| Sufficient and even lighting allows Canny to detect real gradients from lane markings rather than artificial bright spots | Daytime lighting with even exposure across the frame, no extreme bright or dark regions competing with lane markings | Dusk lighting creates streetlamps and headlights that blow out to pure white, producing very strong false gradients that dominate over the still-dark, gradient-free road surface |
| A relatively clean frame where strong edges within the ROI are road-relevant, not generated by surrounding structures | Open road with sparse vegetation and occasional vehicles. ROI primarily contains road surface, markings, and shoulder | Building edges produce the strongest gradients in the frame and dominate Canny's output; weaker edges like power lines survive primarily through hysteresis chaining onto these strong building edges rather than from their own gradient strength |
| Even without lane markings, the road surface should be distinguishable from its boundary by a detectable edge, allowing boundary-based detection as an alternative approach | Clear shoulder line and a distinct asphalt-to-dirt or asphalt-to-vegetation transition provide a detectable edge independent of lane markings | Road surface transitions directly into footpath and shopfronts with no detectable boundary edge, meaning even a boundary-based approach would have nothing reliable to anchor to | 

## Stage-by-Stage Analysis

### Stage 2 : Grayscale

What it does
Collapses three BGR color channels into a single intensity channel. Each pixel of the frame is now representing brightness, with values from 0 (black) to 255 (white).

What it assumed
Roads are painted with lane markings using high contrast colors, preferably white or yellow against a dark asphalt road surface. The contrast ensures it remains distinguishable when turned into intensity, i.e. a detectable brightness difference between markings and road surface.

What happened on Kathmandu footage
No markings lead to the road surface having a uniform low intensity gray all throughout with no detectable variation. No actual bright spots on the surface itself except noise, as dominant bright areas were street lamps, traffic headlights and backlights, and the dusk sky setting, none being road relevant.

Why it happened technically
Without painted markings, there is no single color or brightness factor to use. Grayscale conversion has nothing relevant to preserve, since the input frame had nothing to contrast the lanes against to begin with. It also removes chromatic contrast, and that matters here because Kathmandu road surfaces, footpaths, and nearby buildings are often similar shades of gray and brown. Even if color were retained, there is minimal chromatic difference to exploit, so grayscale removes the last possible fallback.

Implication for the pipeline
Every downstream stage depends on intensity gradients that originate here. Since grayscale produces no road-surface gradients, every subsequent stage from blur, Canny, ROI, Hough, and so forth is operating on a signal that was never present. The failure is seeded at this stage and propagates forward.

### Stage 3: Gaussian Blur

**What it does**
It smooths the grayscale image by replacing each pixel with a weighted average of its neighbors. The goal is to reduce small intensity spikes so Canny edge detection does not react to random noise.

**What it assumed**
The image contains unwanted high-frequency noise, such as small intensity fluctuations, that can create false edges. Blurring should remove that noise and leave only meaningful structural edges, particularly lane markings.

**What happened on Kathmandu footage**
The blur step did reduce minor clutter, but the road surface itself stayed a flat, low-intensity area with no usable gradients. There was still no lane-relevant signal on the road to preserve.

**Why it happened technically**
Gaussian blur only works when there is noise to smooth. In the Kathmandu footage, the road surface had no real intensity signal from markings or contrast patterns, so there was nothing useful for the blur to act on.

**Implication for the pipeline**
This stage is a denoiser, not a signal creator. Since the upstream problem is missing road signal rather than excessive noise, the pipeline enters Canny edge detection with the same empty road surface it already had.

### Stage 4: Canny Edge Detection

**What it does**
It finds strong intensity gradients in the image and uses two thresholds to decide which gradients are real edges. The result is a binary image where white pixels show the detected edges.

**What it assumed**
Lane markings create sharp gradients between paint and asphalt, so Canny should detect those edges reliably.

**What happened on Kathmandu footage**
Canny found many edges on buildings, power lines, vehicle outlines, and other background structures. The road surface itself stayed black in the output, with no edges detected where lanes would be. On highway footage, the expected lane edges were visible and clean.

**Why it happened technically**
The edge detector is doing what it is designed to do: it follows the strongest gradients. In these frames, the strongest gradients come from clutter around the road, not from the road surface. Since the road has no detectable intensity gradient, Canny has nothing lane-relevant to detect.

**Implication for the pipeline**
This stage makes the missing signal obvious. The binary Canny output shows that there is no road-surface gradient to build on, so the next steps such as ROI selection, Hough transform, and line averaging are all working with a frame where the lane region contains no signal.

### Stage 5: Region of Interest Masking

**What it does**
It applies a triangular mask to the Canny output, keeping only pixels inside the chosen region and setting everything outside to black. The triangle is supposed to cover the lower-center part of the frame where lanes are expected.

**What it assumed**
Lane markings appear in a predictable triangular area in the lower half of the frame. Masking out the sky, buildings, and upper frame should remove irrelevant edges and leave only lane-relevant signal.

**What happened on Kathmandu footage**
The mask did what it was supposed to do for the upper frame. It removed the sky, buildings, and out-of-region clutter. However, the triangle interior was empty because Canny had already produced no edges on the road surface.

**Why it happened technically**
ROI masking is a spatial filter, not a signal recovery tool. It can remove noise from outside the lane region, but it cannot create signal that was never detected. Since Canny produced no road-surface edges, the masked output remained black no matter how well the triangle was positioned.

**Implication for the pipeline**
This stage exposes the core failure: the problem is missing signal, not bad spatial filtering. A perfectly placed ROI still gives an empty output when the upstream stages have no lane signal to pass forward. Adjusting the triangle shape or position cannot fix what is fundamentally absent.

### Stage 6: Hough Line Transform

**What it does**
It converts edge pixels from the ROI output into candidate line segments. The algorithm votes for lines that pass through multiple edge points and returns segments that exceed a minimum threshold.

**What it assumed**
There are enough collinear edge pixels along lane markings for the Hough transform to detect consistent lines. Lane edges should create connected points that build up strong votes.

**What happened on Kathmandu footage**
Hough returned no meaningful lane segments. In some frames it detected the dashboard edge at the bottom of the image, and in others it returned random fragments from whatever noise survived the ROI mask. By contrast, highway footage produced clean, consistent segments along the lane markings.

**Why it happened technically**
Hough requires aligned edge pixels to vote for a line. Because the Canny output inside the ROI was essentially empty for the road surface, there were no collinear points to support lane-shaped lines. The few segments it did detect came from dashboard boundaries or stray noise, not from the road.

**Implication for the pipeline**
With no valid line segments returned, the later averaging stage has nothing lane-relevant to use. Any lines that Hough returns are noise artifacts, not road features. The pipeline is now creating output from fragments that have no geometric relationship to actual lanes.

### Stage 7: Line Averaging and Stabilization

**What it does**
It groups Hough line segments by slope sign — negative slopes for the left lane, positive slopes for the right lane — and averages each group into a single slope and intercept. Those averaged parameters are then converted into two fixed-length lines drawn on the frame.

**What it assumed**
Hough returns multiple segments per lane, all with consistent slope signs and similar intercepts. Averaging those segments should produce a stable, accurate representation of each lane boundary.

**What happened on Kathmandu footage**
The averaging stage received the noise fragments that Hough returned and treated them as lane evidence. It produced confident-looking lines every frame, but those lines were tracking building edges, dashboard boundaries, and random clutter — not actual lanes. The lines jumped around with no stability.

**Why it happened technically**
The averaging function cannot tell lane-relevant segments apart from noise fragments. It groups by slope sign and averages whatever it receives. When the input is noise, it produces a precise average of noise. The pipeline fails confidently rather than failing safely, because it has no mechanism to verify that the result corresponds to real road features.

**Implication for the pipeline**
This is the most dangerous failure mode in the system. A pipeline that produces no output would signal failure clearly. A pipeline that draws confident, stable-looking lines on non-lane features can mislead downstream systems or human observers. The output can look correct while being completely wrong.

## Key Failure Modes

Two distinct failure modes are visible in the pipeline. They have different causes and very different implications for how the system can be improved.

### Failure Mode 1: Signal Discontinuity

This appears on highway footage where the dashed white right lane marking is present. The pipeline detects the dashes when they appear, but it produces no right lane line during the gaps between them. The averaged line therefore flickers from frame to frame.

This failure is recoverable. The signal is present but intermittent. Temporal smoothing, such as carrying the last valid detection across short gaps, would stabilize the output without changing the core pipeline.

### Failure Mode 2: Signal Absence

This is the failure seen throughout the Kathmandu footage. No lane markings exist on the road surface, so no intensity gradient is available for any stage to detect. The pipeline does not fail safely; it draws confident-looking lines by latching onto building edges, dashboard boundaries, and random noise fragments that pass slope filtering.

This failure is not fixable through tuning. The lane signal the pipeline is designed to detect simply does not exist in this environment. Changing thresholds, ROI shape, or Hough parameters cannot create lane signal from a road surface that has none. Addressing this failure requires a fundamentally different detection approach or additional sensing modalities.

## Implications 

This evaluation was not designed to show that classical lane detection fails on Nepali roads. It was designed to understand how and why it fails, and what that reveals about the assumptions baked into the pipeline.

The core finding is simple: the classical lane detection pipeline is built on one foundational assumption. Lane markings exist and create detectable intensity gradients. Every stage from grayscale conversion through line averaging depends on this. When that assumption does not hold, the pipeline does not degrade gracefully. It produces confident, wrong output.

This has two practical consequences. First, tuning thresholds and adjusting parameters cannot fix signal absence. Any system deployed on unstructured roads without lane markings will show Failure Mode 2 regardless of how carefully it is calibrated. Second, the pipeline's confident failure is more dangerous than silent failure for safety-critical applications. A system that draws no lines signals that it has lost tracking. A system that draws wrong lines with confidence gives no such signal.

For roads like those in the Kathmandu footage, a viable detection system would need to use cues beyond lane markings. Possible approaches include road boundary detection using edge contrast between the road and its surroundings, semantic segmentation that identifies drivable surface rather than markings, or sensor fusion incorporating depth or GPS. These are outside the scope of this evaluation, but they represent the logical next step given what we have found here.
